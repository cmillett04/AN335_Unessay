An Overview of the Great Apes

Great Apes and Humans: Our Connection, December 12, 2022

Great Apes as a group belong in the genus Hominidae. Humans share this taxonomic family with three other genera: chimpanzees, gorillas, and orangutans. The species within these groups consist of the animals most genetically similar to humans, and hence, examining their behavior and social structures can provide exceptional insights into that of humans, along with a deeper understanding of our own evolutionary history. It is with this in mind that we turn to the other apes for an overview of each of their behaviors and habitats.

Orangutans

Unlike the other non-human apes, which have black or darker brown fur, the orangutan is well-known for its reddish-orange fur color. Orangutans are the largest living arboreal mammal, and they are spread out across two different islands in Southeast Asia: Borneo and Sumatra (in the nations of Indonesia and Malaysia). There are three species of orangutans: Pongo pygmaeus, Pongo abelii, and Pongo tapanuliesis, the last of which was only recognized as a distinct species in 2017.

Much of orangutan behavior and social structure can be explained in terms of their unpredictable and low food availability. The rainforests in which they live go through periods of mast fruiting followed by long periods of low fruit availability, in which they rely more on fallback foods in their diet. These high fluctuations in fruit availability mean that the apes are spread out and largely solitary, are very sexually dimorphic, have extremely long inter-birth intervals, have a slower life history, and high rates of sexual coercion. A morphological feature unique to orangutans is male bi-maturism, in which adult males can take on one of two different forms: flanged or unflanged. Flanged males have cheek pads, long, loud vocalizations, and are preferred by ovulating females, whereas unflanged males do not have cheek pads, have a higher percentage of forced copulations, and are generally avoided by ovulating females.
